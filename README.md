Subject: I think this USB drive contains a puzzle

Hi,

I found something strange on the file, and I’m sending you everything I managed to copy from it.

There wasn’t much left. The final directory contained only a single file.
The person this belonged to is someone I know. He has always been a real computer enthusiast—the kind of person who notices patterns where everyone else sees noise. He knows his way around systems, files, databases, compression tools, and the sort of obscure technical details most people would simply ignore.He is also a sharp observer, methodical in everything he does, and astronomy has been one of his long-time hobbies.

That is why I don’t think any of this is random.
Next to the Computer was a sheet of paper. It did not look like an ordinary note. It looked more like a fragment of instructions.
Near the top, three words had been written in large letters:

MASTER DETAIL SQL

Below them, the page appeared to divide everything into two layers.

The lower part referred to the ground—places, locations, or possibly a map.
The upper part referred to the sky. I remember seeing descriptions of five groups of stars. There was also something about compression, along with a note saying that data had been hidden in colors.

I am not sure whether the colors referred to flags, pixels, map markings, or something embedded inside an image.
There was also an image of Slovenia on the computer screen, which makes me think the “ground” layer might be connected to locations on the map.

A phone had been left on the desk, with a music application open. On the screen was an image of a broken key, carefully divided into three pieces.
At first, I assumed that the phone and the broken key had nothing to do with the map.

Experience has taught me that the observers rarely left coincidences behind.

I also remember seeing something that resembled an observation schedule—possibly something he had sent to friends.
One note suggested that he had distributed the work evenly, as though different people were expected to observe or process separate parts of the same assignment.
The strangest detail was a clearly circled number:

50

Underneath it, he had written what looked like a loop:

for i = 0 to 49
    G = i mod 15
    S = f(Xi)

Beside it, written very carefully, was this exact sequence:

f(Xi) =
[
  5, 4, 13, 16, 21, 12, 9, 1, 2, 6,
  36, 34, 31, 30, 40, 41, 44, 43, 35, 36,
  53, 55, 57, 61, 64, 63, 58, 57, 54, 56,
  66, 65, 67, 60, 70, 72, 75, 73, 75, 76,
  83, 82, 84, 86, 84, 88, 90, 90, 95, 93
]

Knowing him, I doubt those numbers are accidental.

One more thing stood out, although I honestly have no idea what it means.

A separate line on the page said that the inverse system had not yet been incorporated.
It felt less like an instruction and more like a warning—or perhaps an unfinished reminder. It was as though the main system worked in one direction, while whatever was supposed to run backwards was still missing.A password had also been written on the paper:

VIGENERE

The only file in the final directory was named:

'Slovenia.png'

That is still its name now.

Another note mentioned that the creator had been experimenting with compression—a modern standard, always pushed to its highest level.

One sentence had been underlined:

“The picture may only reveal itself once the pressure is released.”

When I opened the file with IrfanView, I saw only a black background filled with small, downward-facing squares in red, green, and blue.
What makes it even stranger is that the image appeared complete on the computer.

From what I can piece together, the file, the notes, the colors, the map, the stars, the music, and the compression all seem to belong to the same system.

I’m sending it to you because I honestly have no idea what he was trying to build—but now I’m curious too.

If you can make anything of this, please let me know.


Gregor
